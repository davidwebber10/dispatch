# Dispatch v2.5.2 — Mobile modals that scroll, and Settings with its own screen

Three mobile fixes. Two of them turned out to be the same bug.

## The new-thread modal was stuck

On a phone, a modal taller than the screen could not be scrolled. The bottom —
including the **Start new thread** button — was simply unreachable.

The surprise: the modal *already* had `overflow-y: auto`. Two other things
conspired.

The panel was capped at `calc(100dvh - 32px)`, and `dvh` does **not** shrink for
the on-screen keyboard. With the keyboard up, the panel was sized to the whole
viewport while only about half of it was visible — so there was almost no
overflow left to scroll, even though half the form sat underneath the keyboard.

And the backdrop centred with `align-items: center`, which distributes overflow
to *both* sides. A panel taller than the viewport had its top pushed above the
scroll origin, permanently out of reach. With `user-scalable=no` set for
full-bleed mobile, there was no pinch-to-zoom escape hatch either.

Scrolling now happens on the backdrop, and the panel centres with `margin: auto`
— which collapses to zero when there is no free space and centres when there is.
So a **tall modal opens at the top and scrolls; a short one stays centred**, from
one CSS rule rather than any height measurement.

Measured on a forced-tall case (806px panel in a 500px viewport): the panel now
rests at +16px instead of clipping above the fold, with 338px of scroll available
and the submit button reachable.

This is the shared modal shell, so New Thread, New Project, Edit Automation and
the rename dialogs are all fixed together.

## The keyboard opened by itself

The new-thread form autofocused its Name field, which raised the keyboard the
instant the modal appeared — which is precisely what pushed the form underneath
it. The two bugs were one.

Autofocus is now **desktop-only**. On a phone the modal opens quiet, and you tap
the field if you want it.

## Settings has its own screen

Settings used to be a gear in the mobile header that opened the desktop modal —
a 520px box with a horizontally-scrolling tab strip, rendered on a phone.

It is now a fourth item in the bottom rail, presented the way a phone should
present settings: a list of sections, each drilling down to its own full screen
with a back button. Back, and the iOS edge-swipe-back gesture, walk out of it
like any other screen.

Under the hood the sections moved into one shared registry that both the desktop
modal and the mobile screens render, so the two can no longer drift apart — and
the desktop settings modal is unchanged.
